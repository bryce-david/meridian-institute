AI in Identity and Access

Identity is the new perimeter. As organizations moved to cloud and hybrid work, the network edge became meaningless as a security boundary. Who someone is — and whether they are who they claim to be — is now the fundamental security question, and AI is transforming every layer of how that question gets answered.

The Identity Crisis in Modern Security

The dominance of identity-based attacks is not incidental — it is the logical consequence of where security investment went for the last two decades. Organizations hardened their network perimeters, encrypted their traffic, and deployed endpoint protection, so attackers moved to the weakest link: the human with legitimate credentials. Credential theft, session hijacking, pass-the-hash attacks, and MFA fatigue (bombarding a user with authentication prompts until they accidentally approve one) now account for the majority of successful breaches. The Verizon Data Breach Investigations Report has consistently found that more than 80% of breaches involve stolen or compromised credentials — a figure that has held roughly constant even as other attack vectors fluctuated.

The core problem with traditional Identity and Access Management (IAM) systems is that they are fundamentally static and rule-based. They verify identity at the point of login — check the password, check the MFA token, grant access — and then largely trust the session that follows. This model was designed for an era when employees logged in from a known office IP address to access a handful of on-premises applications. It cannot cope with a world where the same user connects from a coffee shop in Berlin at 9 AM and from a corporate VPN in Singapore at 11 AM, accesses a mix of SaaS and on-premises tools, and delegates session tokens to automated workflows.

Static rules cannot detect when legitimate credentials are used maliciously. If an attacker steals a valid username, password, and MFA token — increasingly possible through adversary-in-the-middle phishing proxies like Evilginx — they authenticate successfully and the traditional IAM system sees nothing wrong. The session looks identical to any other legitimate session. This is the gap that AI-driven identity security is built to close.

Behavioral Biometrics and Continuous Authentication

Behavioral biometrics represent a fundamentally different approach to identity verification. Rather than verifying a secret (password) or a possession (hardware token) at a single point in time, behavioral biometrics continuously verify the way a person interacts with a device. This includes keystroke dynamics (the timing, pressure, and rhythm of typing), mouse movement patterns (velocity, acceleration, path characteristics between clicks), touchscreen gesture patterns, and even gait recognition from accelerometer data on mobile devices. These signals combine into a behavioral fingerprint that is extraordinarily difficult to replicate — far harder to steal than a password.

The key word is continuous. A behavioral biometrics system does not check identity once at login; it scores the behavioral plausibility of every interaction throughout a session. If a user's typing rhythm changes abruptly — as it would if a different person took over the keyboard mid-session — the system detects that anomaly and can trigger silent re-authentication, step-up verification, or session termination without disrupting legitimate users. Companies like BioCatch (focused on financial services fraud prevention) and TypingDNA (focused on enterprise authentication) have operationalized these capabilities at scale. BioCatch processes behavioral signals from hundreds of millions of sessions monthly, using AI models trained on billions of behavioral data points to distinguish genuine users from impostors, bots, and malware-controlled sessions.

The technology is not limited to detecting external attackers. Behavioral biometrics can also flag insider threats — employees who authenticate legitimately but whose subsequent behavior diverges from their established baseline in ways that suggest they are acting outside their normal role. This is a capability that no rule-based system can replicate without generating paralyzing false positive rates.

Key Stat

A 2024 study found that behavioral biometrics reduced account takeover fraud by up to 70% at financial institutions that deployed the technology — compared to institutions relying solely on knowledge-based authentication and static MFA. The reduction was most pronounced against sophisticated attackers who had successfully stolen valid credentials and MFA tokens.

AI-Powered Identity Threat Detection

Identity Threat Detection and Response (ITDR) has emerged as a distinct security category addressing the specific challenge of detecting attacks that operate through legitimate identity channels. ITDR platforms ingest signals from identity providers, directory services, endpoint agents, and application logs to build behavioral baselines for every user and service account, then flag deviations that match known attack patterns. CrowdStrike Falcon Identity Protection and Microsoft Entra ID Protection (formerly Azure AD Identity Protection) are among the leading platforms in this space, each processing identity-related telemetry across thousands of customer environments to train detection models.

The specific detections that AI enables would be impossible to replicate with static rules. Impossible travel — a user authenticating from New York at 8 AM and from Tokyo at 9 AM — is a straightforward signal, but AI systems go much further: detecting anomalous access to resources the user has never touched, privilege escalation attempts that don't match the user's historical pattern, service account activity at unusual hours, and lateral movement through internal systems that follows a path inconsistent with legitimate administrative workflows. These signals individually might be explained away; AI models correlate them across identity, endpoint, and network telemetry to produce high-confidence detections that a human analyst would take hours to assemble manually.

The response capabilities in modern ITDR platforms are equally important. Detected threats can trigger automated responses — revoke the session token, force re-authentication, quarantine the device, notify the SOC — without waiting for a human decision. In the context of credential-based attacks that can move from initial access to data exfiltration in minutes, the difference between automated and human-in-the-loop response can be the difference between a contained incident and a reportable breach.

Privileged Access Management and AI

Privileged Access Management (PAM) addresses the most sensitive class of credentials in any organization: accounts with administrative, root, or elevated access to critical systems. A compromised privileged account is catastrophically more damaging than a compromised standard user account — it can access sensitive databases, deploy ransomware across the entire environment, exfiltrate configuration data, and cover its own tracks. PAM platforms like BeyondTrust and CyberArk have long managed the vaulting, rotation, and auditing of privileged credentials. AI extends these capabilities significantly by adding continuous behavioral analytics to privileged sessions.

Where traditional PAM recorded sessions and flagged policy violations, AI-augmented PAM systems analyze privileged user behavior in real time — detecting commands that deviate from a system administrator's historical pattern, database queries that access tables the user has never touched, file access that suggests data staging for exfiltration, and tool usage inconsistent with the stated purpose of a privileged session. Just-in-time (JIT) access provisioning reduces the attack surface by eliminating standing privileged access entirely: administrators request elevated permissions for a specific task, the system grants them for a defined window, and revokes them automatically when the window expires. This means a stolen privileged credential is useful for at most a few minutes rather than indefinitely.

Just-in-Time Access
Privileged credentials are provisioned on demand for specific tasks and time windows, then automatically revoked. There are no standing privileged accounts that persist indefinitely and can be stolen and used at leisure.
Session Analytics
AI models continuously analyze privileged session activity against behavioral baselines. Commands, queries, file operations, and network connections that deviate from established patterns trigger real-time alerts or automated session termination.
Anomaly-Based Revocation
Rather than waiting for policy violations defined in advance, AI systems can revoke privileged access based on behavioral anomalies that match known attack patterns — even if no specific rule was written for that exact behavior.

Deepfake Authentication Attacks and Countermeasures

Biometric authentication — face recognition, voice recognition, fingerprint scanning — was once considered the gold standard of identity verification because biometrics are tied to physical characteristics that cannot be stolen the way a password can. The rise of generative AI has materially undermined this assumption. High-quality AI-generated facial imagery and voice synthesis have become accessible enough that they are now being actively deployed against biometric authentication systems. In 2024, attackers demonstrated AI-generated face bypass techniques against banking facial recognition systems, using synthetic imagery to defeat liveness checks that relied on 2D facial matching without adequate depth or motion analysis. Voice synthesis attacks against voice-based authentication systems have been documented at multiple financial institutions.

The countermeasure landscape has responded with increasing sophistication. Liveness detection — verifying that a biometric sample comes from a live human being rather than a photograph, video, or synthetic rendering — is now a mandatory component of any serious biometric authentication deployment. iBeta provides independent certification for liveness detection systems, testing them against presentation attacks under standardized protocols (ISO 30107-3). Multi-factor liveness combines multiple modalities — requiring a user to perform a random head movement, speak a random phrase, and present a face simultaneously — making synthetic bypass exponentially more difficult. Hardware-backed authentication via the FIDO2 standard (WebAuthn + CTAP) uses cryptographic keys stored in secure hardware enclaves that cannot be extracted or replicated, providing a strong complement to biometrics that is not susceptible to synthetic media attacks.

The deeper challenge is that this is an active arms race. As liveness detection improves, generative AI models are trained to produce synthetic media that defeats the specific detection methods in use. Organizations deploying biometric authentication today need to plan for this escalation and build update cycles into their authentication infrastructure — liveness detection systems that were sufficient in 2022 may not be sufficient in 2026.

NIST Warning

NIST has flagged deepfake-based authentication bypass as a critical emerging threat, noting that face recognition systems without liveness detection are vulnerable to presentation attacks using 2D images or video — attacks that require no specialized hardware and are accessible to low-sophistication adversaries. NIST SP 800-76-2 and the ongoing work of the Face Recognition Vendor Test (FRVT) program specifically address liveness and presentation attack detection as mandatory evaluation criteria.
