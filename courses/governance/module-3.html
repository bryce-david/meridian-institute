US AI Policy

Unlike the European Union, the United States has not yet passed comprehensive federal AI legislation. What exists instead is a patchwork: executive orders, agency guidance, sector-specific rules, and a Congress that has held hundreds of hearings but produced little binding law. Understanding this landscape — and why it looks the way it does — is essential for anyone working with AI in an American context.

The Executive Order on AI (October 2023)

Biden's Executive Order 14110 was the most comprehensive US AI policy action to date. Signed in October 2023, it represented the federal government's first attempt to address AI governance across multiple dimensions simultaneously — safety, security, privacy, equity, workers' rights, and innovation. The scope was deliberately broad because the administration wanted to move before Congress could act, and because the absence of legislation meant an executive order was the most powerful available instrument.

Key provisions included: mandatory safety testing for frontier AI models trained above certain compute thresholds (1026 floating-point operations), a requirement that federal agencies develop AI use policies within 365 days, a directive to NIST to develop AI safety and evaluation standards, and tasking DHS and CISA to assess AI risks to critical infrastructure. The order also addressed immigration pathways for AI talent, watermarking of AI-generated content, and equity considerations in AI deployment by federal agencies.

The EO represented genuine policy ambition constrained by the limits of executive authority. Because Congress had not acted, the order could direct federal agencies but could not impose binding requirements on private sector AI development beyond safety reporting. It functioned primarily as a coordination mechanism across the executive branch and a signal of political intent rather than a regulatory framework with enforcement teeth.

Key provision

The EO required developers of the most powerful AI systems — those trained above 1026 FLOPs — to share safety test results with the federal government before public deployment. This was the first time the US government formally required private AI companies to provide safety information to a government body. However, critics noted it lacked independent enforcement mechanisms and depended substantially on voluntary compliance for provisions affecting the broader industry.

The Trump Administration and AI Deregulation

In January 2025, President Trump revoked EO 14110 on his first day in office and issued a new executive order titled "Removing Barriers to American Leadership in Artificial Intelligence." The new order directed federal agencies to develop AI action plans focused on removing "undue burdens" on AI development, eliminated Biden-era equity requirements in federal AI procurement, and emphasized that the US should lead globally through "freedom from unnecessary government control" rather than precautionary regulation.

The shift represents more than a change in policy details — it reflects a fundamentally different regulatory philosophy. Where the Biden framework treated AI governance as a risk management problem requiring coordinated federal action, the Trump framework treats regulation itself as a primary risk to be managed. This creates a distinctly different US posture from the EU's risk-based approach and has increased regulatory uncertainty for companies that were planning compliance programs around EO 14110's requirements. For organizations operating across jurisdictions, the result is that US federal policy and EU AI Act requirements now point in increasingly divergent directions.

Sector-Specific Federal Regulation

In the absence of comprehensive federal AI legislation, sector regulators are filling the governance gap through their existing authorities. This approach reflects both the limits of congressional inaction and the genuine diversity of AI risks across industries — what constitutes appropriate AI governance in medical devices is necessarily different from what it means in mortgage lending or energy grid management.

The FDA has been the most active sector regulator on AI. By 2024, it had approved over 950 AI-enabled medical devices and published detailed guidance on AI/ML-based software as a medical device (SaMD), including a proposed framework for AI systems that continuously learn after deployment. The FTC has used its unfair and deceptive practices authority to issue warnings about AI-enabled deception, including enforcement actions against companies making unsupported AI capability claims. The CFPB has warned financial institutions that using AI in credit decisions does not exempt them from fair lending law requirements under the Equal Credit Opportunity Act. The SEC has addressed AI use in investment advice, requiring that AI-driven recommendations meet the same fiduciary and suitability standards as human advice.

This sector-by-sector approach produces governance, but not coordination. A company deploying AI across healthcare, finance, and employment simultaneously faces different regulatory frameworks, different reporting requirements, and different standards of care in each domain — with no overarching framework reconciling them.

State-Level AI Legislation

In the absence of federal leadership, US states have become the primary sites of AI legislative activity. The pattern mirrors what happened with data privacy: California led with CCPA, other states followed, and the resulting patchwork eventually created pressure for federal preemption. The same dynamic is now playing out with AI.

Colorado passed the Colorado AI Act in 2024 — the first comprehensive US state AI law — requiring developers and deployers of high-risk AI systems to use reasonable care to protect consumers from known or reasonably foreseeable harms, conduct impact assessments, and provide transparency about AI use in consequential decisions. California's legislature passed SB 1047, which would have required safety testing for frontier AI models above certain thresholds, but Governor Newsom vetoed it in September 2024, citing concerns about regulatory overreach. Texas, Virginia, Connecticut, and at least a dozen other states have pending or recently enacted AI legislation with varying scope and requirements.

The underlying challenge is that state AI laws are not harmonized. Colorado's definition of "high-risk AI" differs from the EU AI Act's, which differs from what Texas is proposing. A company building an AI hiring tool must navigate potentially conflicting obligations across every state where its customers operate — a compliance burden that compounds rapidly as state legislative activity increases.

Compliance fragmentation risk

Companies operating nationally face the prospect of complying with 50 different state AI laws if federal preemption legislation doesn't pass. This compliance burden disproportionately affects smaller organizations that lack the legal resources of large technology companies. It also creates perverse incentives: organizations may avoid deploying beneficial AI applications in high-regulation states simply because the compliance cost exceeds the revenue opportunity — a governance failure that is invisible in the legislative record.

The Congressional Landscape

Congress has held more than 200 AI-related hearings since 2022 — featuring testimony from AI researchers, ethicists, industry executives, and civil society groups — but has passed almost no AI-specific legislation. The Senate Commerce Committee and the Senate Judiciary Subcommittee on AI (Rights, Privacy, Technology, and the Law) have been the primary venues for AI policy discussions. The National AI Initiative Act (2020) and the AI in Government Act (2020) remain the most significant AI-related federal legislation, both predating the current AI era and neither imposing significant regulatory obligations on the private sector.

The AI Safety Institute (AISI) at NIST represents the most institutionalized federal AI governance body produced by the executive order era. Established in 2023, it conducts safety evaluations of frontier AI models, develops testing methodologies, and serves as a coordinating point between government and industry. Under the Biden administration it was positioned as the US equivalent of the UK AI Safety Institute. Its mandate and resourcing under the Trump administration has been subject to revision, creating uncertainty about its long-term role.

Jurisdiction Fragmentation
AI spans nearly every sector of the economy, yet no single congressional committee controls AI policy. Commerce, Judiciary, Intelligence, Armed Services, Finance, and HELP committees all have jurisdiction over AI-related issues, producing competing and uncoordinated legislative activity.
Industry Lobbying
Major AI companies have invested heavily in Washington presence. OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, Meta, and Amazon collectively employ hundreds of lobbyists and have spent tens of millions on federal lobbying. This shapes what legislation gets drafted, what amendments pass, and which provisions are softened before floor votes.
Partisan Division
AI regulation has been partially mapped onto existing partisan divisions: Democrats have generally been more open to precautionary regulation emphasizing rights and safety; Republicans have generally framed regulation as a threat to US competitiveness and innovation. Bipartisan compromise has been achievable on specific issues (child safety, national security) but not on comprehensive governance frameworks.
Technical Complexity
Congressional staff lack the capacity to draft technically precise AI legislation. Understanding the difference between a large language model and a traditional ML classifier, or between training compute and inference compute, requires domain expertise that most Hill offices do not have in-house. This creates dependence on industry-provided expertise that disadvantages independent regulatory design.
